# Writing Rules

The article should read like a practitioner in the site's niche wrote it, not
a generic assistant describing the niche. A repo-local style guide overrides
anything here.

## Zero-tolerance patterns

- Long dash characters (em/en dashes). Use a hyphen or rewrite the sentence.
- "Delve", "landscape", "tapestry", "leverage" as a verb, "utilize", "robust",
  "comprehensive", "streamlined", "game-changing", "revolutionary",
  "cutting-edge", "seamless".
- "Let's dive in", "it's worth noting", "it's important to note", "in today's
  world", "at the end of the day", "here's the thing".
- A rhetorical question followed by its immediate answer.
- Identical section structure repeated across every list item (same opener
  style, same rhythm, same bold labels, same callback closer).
- Fake enthusiasm or press-release brand voice.

## Prefer

- Specific examples from the site's domain: named tools, features, numbers,
  real user problems — the domain equivalent of "name the spell, not 'a
  spell'".
- A take a real practitioner could disagree with.
- Paragraph and section length that varies with the idea.
- Direct answers near the top of sections (SEO/AEO extraction).
- Product mentions woven into evidence and examples, not dumped into a sales
  block at the end.

## SEO basics

- One primary keyword plus a small set of related terms.
- Primary keyword in: title, first paragraph, meta description, slug, and at
  least two natural H2s.
- Meta description under 160 characters; SEO title under 60 when possible.
- Internal links to related posts and site pages.
- Descriptive image alt text.
- No keyword stuffing.

## AEO basics (AI answer engines need extractable answers)

- A direct answer sentence near the top of each relevant section.
- Define entities clearly the first time they appear.
- Use explicit comparison language when comparing tools or approaches.
- FAQ sections for high-intent questions, mirrored in frontmatter `faq`.
- Concrete examples and numbers when available.

## Trust signals

- Name real things (products, versions, features, scenarios) instead of
  abstractions.
- Explain how something was tested instead of claiming authority.
- Disclose bias when the site's own product is part of a comparison.

## Quality bar

An **A-grade** draft has a real angle, opens with a specific scene/stat/
problem/take, answers the search intent quickly, uses concrete domain
examples, covers SEO metadata + internal links + FAQ, mentions the product
only where it adds evidence or utility, and passes the zero-tolerance list.

A draft that is "helpful but bland" — good structure, generic content — is a
**B** at best. One strong post with a point of view beats several acceptable
posts that sound like templates. If the draft grades below A, sharpen it
before handing off.
